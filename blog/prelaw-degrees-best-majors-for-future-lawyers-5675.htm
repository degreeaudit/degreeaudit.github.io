Pre-Law Degrees: Best Majors for Future Lawyers

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Choosing the right undergraduate major is a critical decision for aspiring lawyers. While there’s no single "best" pre-law degree, certain disciplines provide skills that align closely with legal education and practice. In today’s rapidly evolving world, where technology, globalization, and social justice dominate headlines, future lawyers must equip themselves with versatile knowledge. Here’s a deep dive into the best majors for pre-law students, considering contemporary global challenges.

Why Your Undergraduate Major Matters

Law schools don’t require specific majors, but they value candidates who demonstrate strong analytical reasoning, critical thinking, and persuasive communication—skills honed in various fields. The American Bar Association (ABA) emphasizes that students should pursue a major that challenges them intellectually and fosters these competencies.

Core Skills for Law School Success

  • Analytical Reasoning: Breaking down complex arguments.
  • Critical Thinking: Evaluating evidence and constructing logical conclusions.
  • Writing Proficiency: Crafting clear, concise, and persuasive documents.
  • Research Abilities: Navigating vast amounts of information efficiently.

Top Pre-Law Majors for Aspiring Lawyers

1. Political Science

A classic choice, political science delves into government structures, legal systems, and public policy—key areas for future attorneys.

Why It’s Relevant Today

  • Global Conflicts: Understanding international relations is crucial amid rising geopolitical tensions (e.g., Ukraine, Taiwan).
  • Policy Debates: Climate change, healthcare, and AI regulation require legal expertise.

Key Courses

  • Constitutional Law
  • International Relations
  • Public Policy Analysis

2. Philosophy

Philosophy sharpens logical reasoning and ethical analysis, making it a powerhouse major for law school.

Why It’s Relevant Today

  • AI Ethics: As artificial intelligence advances, lawyers must grapple with moral dilemmas (e.g., algorithmic bias).
  • Human Rights: Philosophers like John Rawls inform debates on equality and justice.

Key Courses

  • Logic and Argumentation
  • Ethics
  • Jurisprudence

3. Economics

Economics teaches students to analyze data, predict outcomes, and understand market behaviors—skills vital for corporate law or policy work.

Why It’s Relevant Today

  • Tech Antitrust Cases: Big Tech monopolies (e.g., Google, Meta) face global scrutiny.
  • Climate Economics: Carbon pricing and green energy incentives require legal frameworks.

Key Courses

  • Microeconomics
  • Game Theory
  • Law and Economics

4. History

History majors excel at contextualizing events, identifying patterns, and constructing narratives—essential for litigation and legal research.

Why It’s Relevant Today

  • Reparations Movements: Historical injustices (e.g., slavery, colonialism) fuel legal claims.
  • Constitutional Originalism: Debates over the U.S. Constitution’s interpretation hinge on historical analysis.

Key Courses

  • Legal History
  • Comparative Revolutions
  • Civil Rights Movements

5. English/Literature

Strong writers thrive in law, where drafting contracts, briefs, and motions is routine. Literature also fosters empathy, crucial for client advocacy.

Why It’s Relevant Today

  • Disinformation: Lawyers combat fake news and defamation in the digital age.
  • Storytelling in Court: Persuasive narratives win cases (e.g., #MeToo trials).

Key Courses

  • Rhetoric and Composition
  • Critical Theory
  • Narrative Nonfiction

6. STEM Fields (Science, Technology, Engineering, Math)

With tech shaping modern law, STEM graduates bring invaluable expertise to patent law, cybersecurity, and bioethics.

Why It’s Relevant Today

  • AI and Privacy Laws: GDPR, facial recognition bans, and ChatGPT lawsuits demand tech-savvy lawyers.
  • Environmental Science: Climate litigation (e.g., against oil companies) relies on scientific data.

Key Courses

  • Computer Science Fundamentals
  • Environmental Policy
  • Intellectual Property Law (elective)

Emerging Hot-Button Issues and Complementary Majors

1. Environmental Studies

As climate crises escalate, environmental lawyers are in high demand.

Key Focus Areas

  • Renewable energy contracts
  • International climate agreements (e.g., Paris Accord)

2. Criminal Justice

Police reform and mass incarceration debates make this major timely.

Key Focus Areas

  • Bail reform advocacy
  • Forensic science in court

3. International Relations

Globalization and migration crises require cross-border legal expertise.

Key Focus Areas

  • Refugee rights
  • Trade wars and sanctions

How to Choose Your Major: Practical Tips

Align with Your Interests

Passion drives success. If you love STEM, don’t force yourself into political science just because it’s "traditional."

Diversify Your Skill Set

Pair a humanities major with a minor in data science, or vice versa. Law schools adore interdisciplinary backgrounds.

Gain Real-World Experience

Intern at law firms, NGOs, or government agencies to test-drive legal specialties.

Debunking Pre-Law Myths

Myth 1: "You Must Major in Pre-Law"

False. Few schools offer pre-law, and it’s not a prerequisite.

Myth 2: "Hard Majors Boost Your Chances"

Law schools value rigor but prioritize GPA. Don’t tank your grades in quantum physics if it’s not your strength.

Myth 3: "STEM Majors Have an Edge"

While unique, STEM alone won’t compensate for weak LSAT scores or writing skills.

Final Thoughts: The Future of Legal Education

The legal field is evolving, and so should pre-law preparation. Whether you major in philosophy or computer science, focus on building adaptable skills. The best lawyers don’t just understand the law—they understand the world.
