The Importance of Networking During Your Quantity Surveyor Degree

Home / Blog / Blog Details

In today’s hyper-connected world, earning a degree in quantity surveying is about more than just mastering cost estimation, contract management, and construction law. While technical skills are undeniably crucial, the ability to build and leverage professional relationships can make the difference between a mediocre career and an extraordinary one. Networking isn’t just a buzzword—it’s a career accelerator, especially in an industry as relationship-driven as construction and infrastructure development.

Why Networking Matters for Future Quantity Surveyors

The Hidden Job Market

Did you know that up to 80% of jobs are never publicly advertised? Many opportunities are filled through referrals, internal recommendations, or word-of-mouth. As a quantity surveying student, your network can be the bridge to internships, graduate roles, and even future promotions.

Industry Insights Beyond the Classroom

Textbooks teach theory, but networking connects you to real-world challenges. Conversations with professionals can reveal emerging trends like sustainable construction, digital twins, and AI-driven cost estimation tools—topics that might not yet be in your curriculum.

Mentorship and Guidance

A strong network provides access to mentors who can offer career advice, review your CV, or even introduce you to key decision-makers. Many successful quantity surveyors credit their growth to mentors they met early in their careers.

How to Build Your Network as a Student

1. Join Professional Organizations

Organizations like the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS) or the American Society of Professional Estimators (ASPE) host events, webinars, and student chapters. Membership often includes access to exclusive job boards and networking forums.

2. Attend Industry Conferences and Workshops

Events like The International Cost Engineering Council (ICEC) Summit or local construction expos are goldmines for networking. Don’t just attend—engage. Ask questions, exchange business cards, and follow up with LinkedIn connections.

3. Leverage Social Media and LinkedIn

LinkedIn isn’t just for job hunting. Join groups like "Young Quantity Surveyors Worldwide" or "Construction Professionals Network." Share articles, comment on posts, and message professionals for informational interviews.

4. Participate in University Clubs and Competitions

Many universities have construction societies, case study competitions, or guest lecture series. These events often attract industry leaders looking for fresh talent.

5. Internships and Part-Time Work

Even a short internship can expand your network exponentially. Treat every colleague—whether a junior engineer or a senior project manager—as a potential long-term contact.

The Global Perspective: Networking in a Post-Pandemic World

The pandemic reshaped networking, making virtual connections more important than ever. Tools like Zoom and Microsoft Teams have broken geographical barriers, allowing students to connect with professionals worldwide.

Remote Networking Tips

  • Be proactive in virtual events: Turn on your camera, ask questions, and use chat features.
  • Follow up strategically: A personalized LinkedIn message referencing a discussion can leave a lasting impression.
  • Create digital credibility: Publish articles or share insights on platforms like Medium or LinkedIn to showcase your expertise.

Overcoming Common Networking Challenges

"I’m Introverted—How Do I Network?"

Networking doesn’t mean forcing small talk with strangers. Start with low-pressure environments like online forums or small study groups. Prepare a few conversation starters (e.g., "What’s the biggest challenge you’ve faced in cost management?").

"What If I Don’t Have Experience to Offer?"

You don’t need decades of experience to add value. Fresh perspectives, enthusiasm, and a willingness to learn are highly valued. Offer to assist with research or volunteer at industry events.

"How Do I Maintain Connections?"

Networking isn’t a one-time activity. Send occasional updates ("Just passed my RICS assessment!"), congratulate contacts on their achievements, or share relevant industry news.

The Long-Term Payoff

Networking isn’t just about landing your first job—it’s about building a career ecosystem. The connections you make today could lead to:
- Collaborations on international projects
- Invitations to speak at conferences
- Business partnerships or consultancy opportunities

In an era where climate change, urbanization, and tech disruption are reshaping construction, the quantity surveyors who thrive will be those who combine technical excellence with a robust professional network. Start building yours now—your future self will thank you.
