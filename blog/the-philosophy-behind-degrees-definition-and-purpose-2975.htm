The Philosophy Behind Degrees: Definition and Purpose

Home / Blog / Blog Details

In today’s rapidly evolving world, the value and purpose of academic degrees are frequently debated. From skyrocketing tuition costs to the rise of alternative education models, the traditional concept of a degree is being scrutinized like never before. What does a degree truly represent? Is it a ticket to financial stability, a symbol of intellectual achievement, or an outdated relic in the age of digital learning?

The Historical Evolution of Degrees

From Medieval Guilds to Modern Universities

Degrees originated in medieval Europe as certifications granted by guilds and early universities. The term "degree" itself stems from the Latin gradus, meaning "step" or "level." Back then, a degree was a formal recognition of mastery in fields like theology, law, or medicine. Fast forward to the 21st century, and degrees have expanded into hundreds of specializations, from computer science to gender studies.

The Industrial Revolution and the Standardization of Education

The Industrial Revolution transformed education into a system designed to produce skilled workers. Universities became factories of human capital, and degrees served as quality assurance for employers. This shift cemented the idea that a degree was essential for economic mobility—a belief that persists today, despite growing skepticism.

The Modern Purpose of a Degree

Economic Mobility vs. The Debt Crisis

For decades, a college degree was synonymous with higher earning potential. However, the student debt crisis—exceeding $1.7 trillion in the U.S. alone—has forced many to question whether the ROI (return on investment) still justifies the cost. While STEM graduates often secure high-paying jobs, liberal arts majors may struggle to repay loans, fueling debates about the "worth" of certain degrees.

Credential Inflation and the Job Market

Employers increasingly demand degrees for roles that previously didn’t require them, a phenomenon known as credential inflation. This has created a paradox: while degrees are more accessible than ever, their value in the job market is diluted. Some argue that skills-based hiring (e.g., coding bootcamps, certifications) should replace degree requirements, especially in tech-driven industries.

Degrees in the Age of Disruption

The Rise of Alternative Education

Platforms like Coursera, Udemy, and even TikTok tutorials challenge the monopoly of traditional degrees. Micro-credentials, nanodegrees, and digital badges offer flexible, affordable ways to gain expertise without a four-year commitment. Yet, skeptics question whether these alternatives carry the same prestige or long-term career benefits.

The Globalization of Education

Elite Western universities still dominate global rankings, but institutions in Asia and the Middle East are gaining traction. Meanwhile, remote learning erases geographical barriers, allowing students in developing countries to access Ivy League courses online. Does this democratize education, or does it reinforce existing inequalities?

The Philosophical Debate: Intrinsic vs. Instrumental Value

Knowledge for Knowledge’s Sake

Advocates of the liberal arts argue that degrees should cultivate critical thinking, creativity, and civic engagement—not just job skills. In an era of misinformation, these "soft skills" may be more vital than ever.

Degrees as Social Currency

On the flip side, degrees often function as class signifiers. Elite institutions like Harvard or Oxford confer not just education but social capital, networking opportunities, and lifelong prestige. This perpetuates cycles of privilege, raising ethical questions about meritocracy.

The Future of Degrees

Will Degrees Become Obsolete?

With AI automating jobs and the gig economy redefining work, some predict the decline of traditional degrees. Others believe hybrid models—combining formal education with experiential learning—will prevail.

Reimagining Higher Education

Innovators are experimenting with income-share agreements (ISAs), where students pay tuition as a percentage of future earnings, and competency-based programs that prioritize skills over seat time.

The philosophy behind degrees is far from settled. As society grapples with automation, inequality, and the meaning of success, the definition and purpose of degrees will continue to evolve—whether we’re ready for it or not.
