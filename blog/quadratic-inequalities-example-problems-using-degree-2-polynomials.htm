Quadratic Inequalities: Example Problems Using Degree 2 Polynomials

Home / Blog / Blog Details

The language of mathematics is not confined to textbooks; it is the silent code running beneath the surface of our global society. Among its most potent dialects is the quadratic inequality. While often introduced as a simple exercise in finding where a parabola sits above or below the x-axis, the form ax² + bx + c > 0 or its counterparts (<, ≥, ≤) are powerful tools for modeling thresholds, limits, and tipping points. In an era defined by climate crises, economic volatility, and public health frontiers, understanding quadratic inequalities provides a vital lens through which to analyze the nonlinear challenges of our time.

The Foundation: Parsing the Parabola

A quadratic inequality, such as x² - 5x + 6 < 0, asks a profound question: for what range of inputs does the entire system output a value less than zero? The solution is never a single point, but an interval—a continuous set of possibilities. The process is methodical:

  1. Find the Roots: Solve the corresponding equation x² - 5x + 6 = 0. This factors to (x - 2)(x - 3) = 0, giving critical points at x = 2 and x = 3. These are the parabola's x-intercepts, the boundaries where the output is exactly zero.
  2. Analyze the Intervals: The roots divide the number line into three distinct regions: x < 2, 2 < x < 3, and x > 3.
  3. Test and Conclude: By testing a sample point from each interval in the original inequality, we determine where the condition holds true. Here, the parabola (which opens upward) is negative between its roots. Thus, the solution is 2 < x < 3.

This simple framework of boundary points and interval testing becomes extraordinarily powerful when we translate x and y into real-world variables.

Modeling Planetary Boundaries: Climate and Carbon

One of the most urgent applications is in climate science. Consider a simplified model for a region's average temperature increase, T (in °C), as a function of cumulative CO₂ emissions, E (in gigatons): T(E) = 0.00001E² + 0.005E + 0.8.

International accords aim to keep warming below a critical threshold, say 2°C. This sets up a quadratic inequality: 0.00001E² + 0.005E + 0.8 ≤ 2 Rewriting in standard form: 0.00001E² + 0.005E - 1.2 ≤ 0.

Solving for a Safe Emission Budget

Using the quadratic formula, we find the roots (where temperature increase equals exactly 2°C) at approximately E ≈ 200 and E ≈ -600 (we discard the negative root as physically irrelevant). Analysis of the upward-opening parabola reveals the inequality ≤ 0 holds true between the roots. This yields a powerful, non-linear insight: to stay under 2°C of warming, cumulative emissions E must be less than approximately 200 gigatons. This creates a finite "carbon budget," a concept central to IPCC reports. The inequality doesn't just give a target; it defines the entire range of admissible emission pathways (0 < E < 200). Exceeding this parabolic boundary risks catastrophic, nonlinear feedback loops.

The Economics of Inequality and Social Stability

Quadratic models also illuminate socioeconomic dynamics. Let's model a societal "Stability Index," S, scaled from 0 (unrest) to 100 (high stability), against a measure of wealth disparity, G (a Gini coefficient-like metric where 0 is perfect equality and 1 is maximum inequality). Empirical data often suggests an inverted U-shaped relationship (a downward-opening parabola): some inequality may incentivize growth, but beyond a point, it destabilizes society.

A hypothetical model: S(G) = -150G² + 120G + 40. Policymakers might determine that a Stability Index below 50 is dangerously low. To find the disparity levels that trigger this, we solve: -150G² + 120G + 40 ≥ 50 → -150G² + 120G - 10 ≥ 0.

Finding the Window of Feasibility

Multiplying by -1 and reversing the inequality: 150G² - 120G + 10 ≤ 0. The roots are approximately G ≈ 0.09 and G ≈ 0.71. Since this is an upward-opening parabola, the ≤ 0 condition is satisfied between the roots. The solution: 0.09 ≤ G ≤ 0.71.

This result is profound. It suggests that stability is maintained not at a single point, but within a range of permissible inequality. Disparity too low (G < 0.09) might theoretically correlate with other issues (lack of innovation incentive in this model), while disparity too high (G > 0.71) leads to social fracture. The quadratic inequality defines the narrow, nonlinear path a society must navigate to preserve cohesion—a pressing concern in today's world of growing wealth gaps.

Epidemiology and the Herd Immunity Threshold

The fight against pandemics is inherently nonlinear. The basic reproduction number, R₀, for a disease is famously modified by vaccination. The effective R is modeled as R = R₀(1 - v), where v is the proportion vaccinated. Herd immunity is achieved when R ≤ 1.

However, what if vaccine efficacy, e, is not 100%? Then the effective proportion immune is v * e. The condition becomes: R₀(1 - v*e) ≤ 1. Rearranging: R₀ - R₀ e v ≤ 1 → -R₀ e v ≤ 1 - R₀ → v ≥ (1 - 1/R₀)/e.

This is linear in v, but let's introduce a quadratic reality: vaccine uptake often faces hesitancy that grows as vaccination campaigns intensify. We can model a "challenge factor" c that reduces effective coverage: v_effective = v - c v². The inequality becomes: R₀[1 - e(v - c v²)] ≤ 1.

Solving the Vaccination Coverage Puzzle

This simplifies to: R₀ e c v² - R₀ e v + (R₀ - 1) ≤ 0. For a disease with R₀ = 3, e = 0.95, and a challenge factor c = 0.1, we get: 0.285 v² - 2.85 v + 2 ≤ 0. Solving this quadratic inequality yields roots at approximately v ≈ 0.76 and v ≈ 9.24 (nonsensical for a proportion). The upward-opening parabola is ≤ 0 between its roots. Thus, the practical solution is v ≥ 0.76.

This reveals a critical public health insight: due to the nonlinear effect of hesitancy (the c v² term), simply aiming for a linear threshold is insufficient. The campaign must achieve at least 76% uptake to overcome the growing resistance and actually push the effective R below 1. This models the precise struggle health agencies faced during the COVID-19 pandemic in reaching community-protecting coverage.

From Abstract to Action

These examples illustrate that quadratic inequalities are far more than algebraic curiosities. They are frameworks for reasoning about limits in a nonlinear world. The process—define the model, set the critical threshold, find the boundary roots, determine the valid interval—is a universal algorithm for navigating complex systems.

Whether it's determining the maximum allowable pollution, the minimum resource investment for a project's success, or the breaking point of a financial market under stress, the parabola and its inequalities help us map the safe zones from the danger zones. In an age of exponential change and tipping points, the ability to think in these terms—to find not just an answer, but a range of viable solutions bounded by critical curves—is an essential form of literacy. The next time you see a graph of a parabola, see it not just as a curve, but as a map of possibility, a divider of fates, and a guide for steering our collective future through the turbulent, nonlinear landscape of the 21st century.
