How to Verify the Authenticity of a Degree Before Hiring

Home / Blog / Blog Details

The landscape of hiring has been fundamentally reshaped. The rise of remote work, the gig economy, and a highly competitive talent market have created unprecedented opportunities for businesses to tap into a global pool of candidates. Simultaneously, these very advancements have opened the floodgates to a less welcome phenomenon: a dramatic surge in credential fraud. In today's hyper-connected world, the simple act of trusting a resume at face value is not just naive; it's a significant business risk. The prevalence of "diploma mills"—unaccredited institutions that sell degrees for a flat fee with little to no academic work—and outright forged documents has made pre-employment degree verification an essential, non-negotiable step in the hiring process.

The consequences of hiring based on a fraudulent degree are severe and multifaceted. They extend far beyond mere embarrassment. Financially, you risk the cost of a bad hire—recruitment expenses, training time, and eventual termination and re-hiring processes. Operationally, an unqualified employee can lead to project failures, decreased team morale, and reputational damage if their incompetence affects client work. Legally, in certain regulated industries like healthcare, finance, or engineering, employing someone without the mandatory credentials can result in massive fines, loss of licensure, and devastating lawsuits. In an era where corporate integrity is under a microscope, ensuring your team is genuinely qualified is the bedrock of sustainable success.

The Red Flags: Spotting Potential Fraud Before You Even Verify

Before initiating a formal verification process, a keen eye during the resume screening and interview stages can save valuable time and resources. Certain inconsistencies often serve as early warning signs of credential falsification.

Inconsistencies in Dates and Details

Scrutinize the timeline. Does the candidate's graduation date logically align with their work history? Is there an overlap where they claim to have been a full-time student and a full-time employee in a demanding role? Look for vague references to their field of study or a degree that seems oddly generic for the claimed institution.

The Language of Evasion

Pay close attention to how the candidate discusses their education. In an interview, do they become noticeably vague or deflect questions about their university experience, specific projects, or favorite professors? An authentic graduate can usually recall specific details, courses, or formative academic experiences, while a fraudster will often rely on rehearsed, high-level platitudes.

Unfamiliar or Unverifiable Institutions

Be wary of universities you have never heard of, especially those with impressive-sounding but generic names that mimic prestigious institutions. A common tactic is to use a name that is very similar to a well-known university but with a slight variation. If the institution's name raises a doubt, it's a clear signal that deeper verification is needed.

The Verification Toolkit: Proven Methods to Confirm Credentials

Once a candidate has passed the initial screening and you are considering an offer, a systematic verification process must be implemented. The following methods form a comprehensive toolkit for authenticating academic credentials.

1. The Direct Approach: Requesting Official Documents

The first and most straightforward step is to ask the candidate to provide official documentation.

  • Official Transcripts: An official transcript, often sent directly from the registrar's office to you in a sealed envelope or via a secure digital portal, is a powerful piece of evidence. It not only confirms the degree but also provides a detailed record of courses taken and grades achieved. Unofficial copies are easily altered and should not be considered sufficient proof.
  • Diploma Copy: While a diploma can be forged, asking for a high-quality copy is still a useful step. Examine it for official seals, signatures, and the overall quality of the document. Compare it to a known genuine diploma from the same institution if possible.

It is crucial to obtain the candidate's explicit written consent before proceeding with any verification. This is not only a best practice but also a legal requirement in many jurisdictions to comply with data privacy laws like the GDPR in Europe or the CCPA in California.

2. The Gold Standard: Third-Party Background Screening Services

For most medium to large-sized companies, partnering with a professional background check company is the most efficient and reliable method. These firms specialize in verification and have established relationships with thousands of educational institutions worldwide.

  • How They Work: You provide the candidate's information (with their consent), and the screening service acts as your agent to contact the institution's registrar or records office. They verify the dates of attendance, the type of degree earned, and sometimes even the field of study and honors received.
  • Benefits: This method saves your HR team immense time and effort. These services also understand the specific protocols and potential roadblocks for different universities, both domestic and international, making them highly effective.

3. The Direct Line: Contacting the Educational Institution Yourself

If you prefer a hands-on approach or are verifying a critical role, you can contact the institution directly.

  • Finding the Right Department: The correct point of contact is almost always the Office of the Registrar or the Student Records office. Avoid admissions or general inquiry numbers, as they typically cannot release student information.
  • The Process: You will need to provide the candidate's full name (as it would appear on their student record), their date of birth, student ID number (if available), and the years of attendance. The institution will also require a signed release form from the candidate authorizing the disclosure of their information. Be prepared for processing fees and potential delays, as many registrars are overwhelmed with such requests.

Navigating Complex Scenarios

Not all verification processes are straightforward. Modern hiring presents unique challenges that require adaptable strategies.

International Degree Verification

Verifying degrees from outside your home country adds layers of complexity. Language barriers, different academic systems, and data privacy laws can be significant hurdles.

  • Use a Specialized Service: This is the most recommended path. Many third-party screeners have international capabilities and partners.
  • Credential Evaluation Services: Organizations like World Education Services (WES) or Educational Credential Evaluators (ECE) can be invaluable. They not only verify the authenticity of the foreign credential but also provide a U.S. equivalency (e.g., stating that a foreign degree is equivalent to a U.S. bachelor's degree), which helps in assessing the candidate's qualifications accurately.

Dealing with Closed Institutions

What if the university the candidate attended has closed down? This doesn't automatically mean the degree is fraudulent. Many legitimate schools have shut their doors over the years.

  • State Archives: In the United States, when a college closes, it is often required to transfer its student records to a state department of education or a designated custodian. A web search for "[State Name] closed school records" can often point you in the right direction.
  • Accrediting Body Records: The accrediting body that originally accredited the institution may also hold or know the location of these records.

Building a Culture of Trust and Verification

Implementing a rigorous verification process is not about fostering an environment of suspicion. On the contrary, it is about building a culture of integrity and trust. By applying the same consistent standard to every candidate for a given role, you demonstrate a commitment to fairness and a dedication to maintaining a qualified workforce.

Make your verification policy clear and transparent from the outset. Include it in your job descriptions and inform candidates during the initial stages that an offer is contingent on the successful completion of a background check, which includes degree verification. Most honest candidates will understand and appreciate that this is a standard business practice designed to protect the company and its employees.

In a world where a few clicks can create a convincing but entirely fake identity, the due diligence of verifying academic credentials is a critical defense. It protects your company's assets, its reputation, and the hard work of every qualified employee on your team. By making degree verification a systematic, non-negotiable part of your hiring workflow, you are not just checking a box; you are making a strategic investment in the foundation of your organization's future.
